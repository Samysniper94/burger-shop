About us

Our product

Whether the word hamburger triggers images of America's corporate world or memories and fantasies of scented meat sizzling on the grill, one thing is for sure: the hamburger is a food with a long, multi-stage history worth exploring. A purely American invention, the name always brings to mind the image of a ground beef medallion between loaves. Today burgers are stacked with delicious toppings that range from simple foods like lettuce, onions, ketchup and mustard to gourmet mixes of epic proportions.

The creation of the hamburger as we know it today has its origins in a time when urban centers were growing and cheap and fast food was needed to feed the masses.

Revolutionary history

Ground beef has been used in a variety of cultures as food although it was never served between buns like today's hamburgers. There are different stories about ground beef turned into sausage, on toast, or uncooked in the form of tartar sauce popularized by Russians who immigrated to Germany. In fact, it wasn't until the 19th century that “Hamburg steak” became a popular menu item in America, and even then, it wasn't served between loaves.

At the beginning of the 19th century, it was not easy for the general public to get meat grinders. Just as infrared cooking was not commercially available or affordable until we launched our infrared Sizzle Zone, hamburger meat was not easy to produce and required intense physical labor.

Hamburg steak

Anyone wishing to serve a Hamburg steak had to first use chisel-like tools to mince the meat. In 1845, numerous shredders appeared on the market and facilitated the production of ground beef in a way more similar to what we know today. This also paved the way for other popular ground beef dishes like hot dogs and meatloaf.

Thanks to the magic of language, the Hamburg steak in English morphologically transformed into a hamburger, then the simpler form burger, which is now preceded by prefixes indicating the type of hamburger that is: "cheeseburger" (cheeseburger), "baconburger" (burger with bacon), "mooseburguer" (elk burger), among others.
